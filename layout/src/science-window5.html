The seed of Creative Landscape was 2015’s Hacking Creativity, a research project into the ecology of human creativity, and the largest-ever study of creative habits. Led by human performance expert Andy Walshe, Hacking Creativity enlisted the expertise of creativity researcher Mark Runco, ethnographer Lizbet Simmons, community ecologist Eric Berlow, and computational ecologist Rich Williams, as well as David Gurman, Kaustuv DeBiswas, and Anna Christy. The study sought to understand the variety of ways in which creative people create, not just in one person, but in thousands.

We asked ourselves: could we learn how to nurture creativity in people, learn how to share it, and harness it to help people come up with creative solutions to the world’s hardest challenges?

Fig. 1

Hacking Creativity allowed us to map the tens of thousands of relationships between creative habits, better understanding how people feel most creatively fruitful. We began by asking a select group of 500 highly-successful creative people hundreds of questions, and from their answers, distilled the most pertinent few dozen questions. We shared this more refined survey with tens of thousands of people, mapping the 100,000 connections between their creative styles.

Fig. 1

Participants in the Hacking Creativity project were linked if they shared similar creative habits. Clusters of people that tend to co-share the similar sets of habits were used to define Creative Species in the Creative Landscape.

From the data, we discovered that people roughly fit into one of Seven Creative Species, but, more importantly, we found that there is no single right way to be creative. Highly successful people that included world-renowned inventors, circus performers, chefs, and rocket scientists varied widely in their creative preferences. In fact, the research shows that many of the creative habit recommendations in the popular press are misleading, and sometimes simply incorrect. Our project offers an alternative to the “top ten list” mentality that assumes there’s only one correct suite of creative habits.

Fig. 2

Participants in the broader study of ~10,000 people linked if they share similar creative habits. The colored groups define our 'Creative Species' — or clusters of people that tend to co-share similar sets of habits. The 'defining habits' of each Creative Species were those that were most over-represented in that group relative to the rest of the participants. Note that links across groups show that individuals within one Creative Species often also have some affinities to other Species.

Fig. 2

Creative Landscape is how we begin to share what we learned with a wider audience. We believe that by better understanding your creative style, and the styles of others, you can better harness the transformative power of creativity in your life and work, and also better collaborate with those who have different creative styles.

We chose to express these insights through the metaphor of the natural world because, like all living things, creativity is also a type of dynamic ecosystem. Every species on earth is a unique creative solution to the universal problem of life — how to survive, grow, and reproduce. Every unique combination of strategies for solving this problem has its own advantages and weaknesses. Just as a Cactus won’t grow well in the rainforest, and a Water Lily won’t thrive in the desert, by understanding your own unique suite of creative strengths, you can cultivate the best strategy toward greater creativity.
